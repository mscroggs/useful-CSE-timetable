Avoiding Order Reduction with Explicit Runge-Kutta Exponential Methods in Nonlinear Initial Boundary Value Problems
Begoña Cano
Thursday, March 2 2:35 PM–2:50 PM
This is the 1st talk in Innovations in Time Integration of Stiff Systems of Differential Equations - Part II of II (2:35 PM–4:15 PM)
G101

A technique will be shown to recover the classical order of the method when explicit exponential Runge-Kutta methods integrate reaction-diffusion problems. In the literature, methods of high enough stiff order for problems with vanishing boundary conditions have been constructed, but that implies restricting the coeffcients and thus, quite probably, the computational cost. In contrast, the technique being suggested is cheaper because it just needs, for any method, to add some terms with information on the boundaries. Moreover, time-dependent boundary conditions will be directly tackled.

Emerging Time Integration Methods for Atmospheric Modeling
Stéphane Gaudreault
Thursday, March 2 2:55 PM–3:10 PM
This is the 2nd talk in Innovations in Time Integration of Stiff Systems of Differential Equations - Part II of II (2:35 PM–4:15 PM)
G101

Exponential time integrators are a very accurate approach for stiff systems of differential equations. In recent years, many advances have been made to improve their computational efficiency. In numerical weather prediction models, it is common to use implicit or semi-implicit integrators with large time steps to reduce the cost of the solution. Exponential methods have the advantage of allowing time steps as large as the implicit schemes, while correctly simulating all relevant wave dispersions. In this presentation, we investigate the potential of different exponential schemes in the context of atmospheric modelling using idealised test cases of the shallow water and Euler equations.

High-Order Diagonally Implicit Runge-Kutta Schemes for Stiff and Oscillatory Problems
Yousef Alamri
Thursday, March 2 3:15 PM–3:30 PM
This is the 3rd talk in Innovations in Time Integration of Stiff Systems of Differential Equations - Part II of II (2:35 PM–4:15 PM)
G101

Fully implicit Runge-Kutta (RK) schemes possess superior stability properties over explicit RK schemes. Thus, they are employed to accurately integrate stiff or oscillatory systems of ordinary differential equations or semidiscretizations of partial differential equations. However, the computational cost of implementing implicit RK methods often limits their applicability, especially when combined with large-scale and high-resolution numerical simulations. As an efficient alternative, diagonally implicit Runge-Kutta (DIRK) schemes are sought. We present an optimization approach for designing DIRK schemes of order six and higher that are A-stable and stiffly accurate. The accuracy and robustness of these schemes are shown via extensive numerical tests on diverse problems. This optimization approach can be adopted for designing numerical integrators with other desired properties.

☆ 3:35 PM–3:50 PM (G101) Jared Stewart, Applications of Exponential Integrators to Chemical Combustion Simulations ▼ Show talk info ▼▲ Hide talk info ▲
Applications of Exponential Integrators to Chemical Combustion Simulations
Jared Stewart
Thursday, March 2 3:35 PM–3:50 PM
This is the 4th talk in Innovations in Time Integration of Stiff Systems of Differential Equations - Part II of II (2:35 PM–4:15 PM)
G101

Exponential integrators have been shown as an effective approach to solve stiff systems of differential equations. These methods possess good stability properties all while offering computational savings over implicit methods for some problems where efficient preconditioners are not available. We will discuss the application of variable time stepping exponential methods of EPIRK-type to chemical combustion problems. These variable time-stepping schemes leverage the KIOPS algorithm to quickly and accurately approximate the exponential-like $\varphi$-functions which constitute the main computational expense of exponential integrators. Combustion problems are notorious for stiffness that arises from both the chemical kinetics portion of the models as well as transport mechanisms driving the process. We demonstrate that even straight-forward, i.e. out-of-the-box, application of exponential methods to these systems yields performance comparable to the implicit methods of CVODE (LLNL) and TrBDF2 (SNL). We carry out a number of experiments using GRI, NHeptane, NButane, NDodecane, and Hydrogen combustion mechanisms to compare performance of exponential and implicit methods over the course of ignition at a single point. Our numerical studies include both overall comparison of the performance of the integrators as well as detailed analysis of underlying computations with Krylov-based techniques.
