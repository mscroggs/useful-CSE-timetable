Recent Advancements of Non-Gaussian Processes in Bayesian Inversion
Lassi Roininen
Wednesday, March 1 9:45 AM–10:00 AM
This is the 1st talk in Statistical Inverse Problems: Theory and Computation - Part I of II (9:45 AM–11:25 AM)
G109

This talk is focused on the recent work, which has considered the promotion of non-Gaussian priors for tasks in inverse problems. This is a challenging area as there are numerous applications where one requires learning rough features and edges, while not having a universal prior that works well for this. As a result this presentation will consider recently proposed prior and their developments, such as analysis and numerical evidence that they outperform Gaussian, and other non-Gaussian priors. This will be tested on a range of inverse problems such as tomography and PDE-inversion.

Modeling and Bayesian Calibration for Tumour Cell Dynamics Under Different Environmental Conditions
Laura Scarabosio
Wednesday, March 1 10:05 AM–10:20 AM
This is the 2nd talk in Statistical Inverse Problems: Theory and Computation - Part I of II (9:45 AM–11:25 AM)
G109

The dynamics of cancer cells is strongly affected by the microenvironment surrounding the tumour. In this talk, I will present a systematic approach to model the influence of different environmental factors on tumour progression, and I will discuss the methodology to calibrate the model within a Bayesian framework. From results using different experimental data sets, we will see how the whole procedure allows to shed some light on the underlying biological mechanisms.

Random Tree Besov Priors
Hanne Kekkonen
Wednesday, March 1 10:25 AM–10:50 AM
This is the 3rd talk in Statistical Inverse Problems: Theory and Computation - Part I of II (9:45 AM–11:25 AM)
G109

We propose alternatives for Bayesian a priori distributions that are frequently used in the study of inverse problems. The aim is to construct priors that have same kind of good edge-preserving properties than total variation or Mumford-Shah but correspond to well defined infinite dimensional random variables and can be approximated with finite dimensional random variables. This is done by introducing a new random variable T that takes values in the space of ‘trees’, and which is chosen so that the realisations of the unknown have singularities only on a small set.

☆ 10:55 AM–11:10 AM (G109) Xinzhu Liang, Randomized MISMC Ratio Estimators ▼ Show talk info ▼▲ Hide talk info ▲
Randomized MISMC Ratio Estimators
Xinzhu Liang
Wednesday, March 1 10:55 AM–11:10 AM
This is the 4th talk in Statistical Inverse Problems: Theory and Computation - Part I of II (9:45 AM–11:25 AM)
G109

We consider the problem of estimating expectations with respect to a target distribution with an unknown normalizing constant, and where even the unnormalized target needs to be approximated at finite resolution. Under such an assumption, we extend a recently introduced multi-index Sequential Monte Carlo (SMC) ratio estimator, which provably enjoys the complexity improvements of multi-index Monte Carlo (MIMC) and the efficiency of SMC for inference. The present work leverages a randomization strategy to remove bias entirely, which simplifies estimation substantially, particularly in the MIMC context, where the choice of index set is otherwise important. With theoretical results, the proposed method provably achieves the same canonical complexity of MSE$^{-1}$ under appropriate assumptions as the original method, but without discretization bias. It is illustrated on examples of Bayesian inverse problems.
