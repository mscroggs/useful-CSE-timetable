Quantum Krylov Methods: Why Do They Work?
Ethan N. Epperly
Wednesday, March 1 4:00 PM–4:15 PM
This is the 1st talk in Quantum Algorithms for Scientific Computing - Part I of II (4:00 PM–5:40 PM)
D503

Quantum Krylov methods are a class of quantum algorithms which have the potential to solve large eigenvalue problems on near-term quantum devices. An essential step in these methods is the solution of an ill-conditioned generalized eigenvalue problem where the matrix pencil has been corrupted by large Monte Carlo errors resulting from quantum measurements. Classical results from numerical linear algebra and matrix perturbation theory fail to explain why and how such ill-conditioned, noise-corrupted eigenvalue problems should be solvable. This talk presents theoretical analysis and algorithmic recommendations which explain this mystery and provide practical guidance for solution of generalized eigenvalue problems in quantum Krylov methods.

Quantum Circuits for Hamiltonian Simulation with Quantum Signal Processing
Nathan Fitzpatrick
Wednesday, March 1 4:20 PM–4:35 PM
This is the 2nd talk in Quantum Algorithms for Scientific Computing - Part I of II (4:00 PM–5:40 PM)
D503

Hamiltonian simulation via quantum signal processing is known to be optimal. In recent years, abstract oracle circuit structures for these types of algorithms have been proposed. However, the best practice for constructing these oracles when using a universal gate set is relatively unclear. In this talk we propose compilation strategies for qubitization which minimise the number of one and two qubit gates. Where we make use of Quantinuumâ€™s pytket and show how we can build up layers of abstraction using circuit boxes to construct the high level circuit oracles. Typically these circuit primitives require many ancillary qubits and hence we use a tensor network quantum simulator to simulate these circuits. Where we show some examples of Hamiltonian simulation on the Hubbard model treated as a molecular wire.
